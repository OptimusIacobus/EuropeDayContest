What is Brexit?

“Brexit” is a word that describes the United Kingdom’s exit from the European Union. June 2016, Britain voted in favor of exiting the European Union. The official date for the exit of Britain is on March 29th 2019. However, it is argued that the United Kingdom can stop the exit through a unilateral decision from its government. The legality of this procedure is disputed, but it allows for the UK a way out if they decide the exit is not the optimal decision. One of the main voting issues was on immigration. Britain was pushing for more nationalistic, stricter immigration policies. While being in the EU, the UK faced a lot of immigration from its neighboring countries. In an attempt to minimize immigration to focus on its own citizens, the UK’s decision for brexit is logical. In terms of the current short-term macroeconomic effects, Britain has seen a negative trend. British income dropped to between 0.6 - 1.3% across the country. UK’s GDP saw a loss of 1.3%, and businesses are facing uncertainty. The long term predictions are similar. The general consensus agrees that through less foreign investment, less immigration, and new trade barriers, Brexit will inevitably cause a decline in UK’s economy.

The Euro Currency:

The Euro is the official currency of 19 EU countries: Austria, Belgium, Cyprus, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Luxembourg, Malta, the Netherlands, Portugal, Slovakia, Slovenia, and Spain. These countries are collected known as the euro area or eurozone, with currency regulation controlled by the Eurosystem, which consists of the European Central Bank in Frankfurt, Germany and the national banks of member nations. It was established as the official EU currency in 1992 through the Maastricht Treaty and remains as the official EU currency.
